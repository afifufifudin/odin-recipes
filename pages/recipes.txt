- Pecel
Pecel: A Symphony of Vegetables in Peanut Sauce
Pecel, a beloved Indonesian dish, is a vibrant salad bathed in a creamy, nutty peanut sauce. It's a celebration of fresh vegetables, each bite bursting with contrasting textures and flavors. Get ready to whip up this vegan delight in minutes!

Ingredients:

For the Peanut Sauce:
150g raw peanuts
50g brown sugar
3 red chilies (adjust for spice preference)
2 shallots, roughly chopped
2 cloves garlic, roughly chopped
1 kaffir lime leaf, torn
1/2 inch ginger, roughly chopped
1/4 cup tamarind paste
1 cup water
Salt to taste

For the Salad:
250g water spinach (kangkung), blanched
150g green beans, blanched
100g bean sprouts, fresh
1 cucumber, thinly sliced
1 hard-boiled egg (optional)
Sambal terasi (optional, for extra oomph)

Instructions:
Make the Peanut Sauce: Toast the peanuts in a dry pan until golden brown and fragrant. Grind them into a coarse powder in a spice grinder or mortar and pestle.
In a pan, heat oil and fry the shallots, garlic, and chilies until fragrant. Add the ginger and kaffir lime leaf, cook for another minute.
Add the ground peanuts, brown sugar, tamarind paste, and water. Bring to a simmer and cook until the sauce thickens and flavors meld. Season with salt to taste.
Assemble the Salad: Arrange the blanched vegetables and cucumber on a plate. Drizzle with the peanut sauce and toss gently. Top with a sliced hard-boiled egg and a dollop of sambal terasi (optional).
Enjoy! Dig in and savor the harmonious blend of textures and flavors. The crunchy vegetables, creamy peanut sauce, and a hint of spice make this a truly unforgettable dish.

Tips:
Feel free to customize your salad with other vegetables like cabbage, carrot, or papaya leaves.
For a richer flavor, you can toast the peanuts and spices in coconut oil.
Leftover pecel tastes even better the next day! Store it in the fridge for up to 2 days.
With its symphony of flavors and textures, pecel is a testament to Indonesian culinary ingenuity. So go ahead, explore the world of pecel, and let your taste buds embark on a delightful adventure!


- Soto ayam
Soto Ayam: Fragrant Indonesian Chicken Noodle Soup
Soto ayam, a soul-warming Indonesian classic, translates to "chicken soup." But it's so much more than that. It's a fragrant symphony of spices, tender chicken, silky noodles, and crunchy toppings, singing in a turmeric-tinged broth. Let's cook up a pot of sunshine!

Ingredients:

For the Yellow Spice Paste:

5 shallots, chopped
3 cloves garlic, chopped
1 inch ginger, chopped
1 inch turmeric, fresh or ground
1 tbsp coriander seeds
1/2 tsp cumin seeds
1/4 tsp white peppercorns
1 candlenut (optional)
1 tbsp vegetable oil
1/4 cup water
For the Broth:

1 whole chicken (around 1.5kg)
1.5L water
1 cinnamon stick
2 star anise
3 lemongrass stalks, white parts only, bruised
3 kaffir lime leaves
1 tsp salt
1/2 cup coconut milk (optional, for richer flavor)
For the Noodles and Toppings:

200g rice vermicelli or yellow noodles
2 hard-boiled eggs, halved
100g bean sprouts
1 lime, cut into wedges
Fried shallots
Chopped cilantro
Sambal oelek (optional)
Instructions:

Make the Spice Paste: Blend shallots, garlic, ginger, turmeric, spices, and candlenut (if using) into a smooth paste. Heat oil in a pan and fry the paste until fragrant and slightly darker. Add water and cook until thickened.

Simmer the Broth: In a large pot, combine chicken, water, spices, salt, and the cooked spice paste. Bring to a boil, then reduce heat and simmer for 1 hour, or until chicken is cooked through. Remove chicken and let cool, then shred the meat.

Assemble the Soup: Strain the broth and return it to the pot. Add coconut milk (if using) and bring back to a simmer. Add noodles and cook until tender.

Serve: Divide noodles and broth among bowls. Top with shredded chicken, halved eggs, bean sprouts, lime wedges, fried shallots, and cilantro. Drizzle with sambal oelek (optional) for a spicy kick.

Tips:

You can substitute chicken thighs for a richer flavor.
Feel free to add other vegetables like carrots, cabbage, or celery to the broth.
Leftover soto ayam tastes even better the next day! Store it in the fridge for up to 3 days.
Embrace the comforting warmth of soto ayam, a dish that speaks of Indonesian culinary heritage. With its fragrant broth, tender chicken, and vibrant toppings, it's a guaranteed way to nourish your body and soul.

-Rawon

Rawon: Indonesia's Nutty, Black Beef Symphony
Rawon, a signature Indonesian dish, isn't just a soup, it's an experience. Its dark, almost black broth, infused with the earthy richness of keluak nuts, surprises the palate with complex, savory flavors. Tender beef, vibrant vegetables, and crunchy sprouts complete this unforgettable culinary masterpiece.

Ingredients:

For the Keluak Paste:

5 keluak nuts (black nuts), roasted, peeled, and mashed (wear gloves when handling!)
4 shallots, chopped
3 cloves garlic, chopped
1 inch ginger, chopped
1/2 tsp cumin seeds
1/4 tsp coriander seeds
1/4 tsp black peppercorns
1 tbsp vegetable oil
For the Broth:

500g beef chuck or brisket, cut into cubes
1.5L water
2 lemongrass stalks, white parts only, bruised
3 kaffir lime leaves
1 cinnamon stick
1/2 tsp salt
1 tbsp brown sugar
1/2 cup tamarind paste
For the Toppings:

150g green beans, blanched
100g bean sprouts
1 hard-boiled egg, halved (optional)
Fried shallots
Chopped cilantro
Sambal terasi (optional)
Instructions:

Make the Keluak Paste: Blend shallots, garlic, ginger, spices, and keluak nuts into a smooth paste. Fry in oil until fragrant and slightly browned.

Simmer the Broth: In a large pot, combine beef, water, spices, salt, brown sugar, and tamarind paste. Bring to a boil, then reduce heat and simmer for 1.5 hours, or until beef is tender. Add the keluak paste and simmer for another 30 minutes, stirring occasionally.

Assemble the Soup: Add blanched green beans and simmer for 5 minutes. Remove beef and set aside.

Serve: Divide broth and beans among bowls. Top with sliced beef, halved egg (optional), bean sprouts, fried shallots, and cilantro. Drizzle with sambal terasi (optional) for extra intensity.

Tips:

If fresh keluak nuts are unavailable, you can use store-bought keluak paste for convenience.
For a lighter broth, you can skip the keluak paste altogether.
Leftover rawon tastes even better the next day! Store it in the fridge for up to 3 days.
Dive into the dark depths of rawon and unlock a universe of flavor. Its unique blend of earthy, savory, and slightly sweet notes will leave you wanting more. So, gather your ingredients, embrace the adventure, and experience the magic of Indonesian cuisine!
